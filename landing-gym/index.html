      03

      House rules

      A marketplace of things that reshape your software is only safe if the things cannot do very much. So they cannot.

      1. A skill has no code in it

        It is a text file: a personality, a list of which built-in tools it may call, and a description of the panels to draw. There is nothing in it that could run.

      2. The tools are ours, and there are not many

        Start a timer. Convert a unit. Edit a list. Do arithmetic. Do date maths. A skill picks from that fixed, audited catalogue or it does without. It cannot invent a tool, reach the network, or touch a file.

      3. One skill leads; the rest assist

        Stack four personalities onto a three-billion-parameter model and you get a confused one. So exactly one skill owns the voice — you choose which — and the others contribute a line, their tools, and their panels.

      4. The meter blocks, or it warns. It never shrugs

        Run out of context and the combination is refused before you enable it, with the name of the plate to drop. Run low on speed and you get an amber warning and the choice. Nothing is silently downgraded behind your back.

      5. Skills cannot bring their own stylesheet

        They pick from the app's design tokens. Twelve skills by twelve authors still look like one program, and every panel is keyboard-operable whether its author thought about that or not.

      6. Nothing goes on the floor untested

        Before a skill is published it has to pass a behavioural exam on the reference model — representative prompts in, expected tool calls and panels out — plus a safety review and a hard budget on how much prompt it is allowed to spend.

      06

      Questions a
      spotter would ask

      So I really can't train it?

      Not in the sense the word usually means. The model's weights are fixed and identical on every machine. A skill changes the instructions it runs under, the tools it can reach, and the interface you see — not what it learned. We call it a gym because you are loading equipment onto something, not because there is a training loop. If you want a model fine-tuned on your data, this is the wrong product and we would rather say so here than in a refund email.

      How many skills can it hold at once?

      There is no fixed number, which is why there is a meter. Each skill spends part of the model's context window — its instructions, its tools, and its panels — and the app holds a fixed reserve back for your live conversation. When the next skill would not fit, the planner refuses it before you enable it and names the plate to drop. That budget is the same on every machine: a stronger bench buys faster replies and fewer speed warnings, not more plates. Toggle the bench above and the speed readout moves while the capacity stays put — that is the real rule, not a marketing number.

      Why does one skill have to lead?

      Because concatenating four personalities onto a three-billion-parameter model produces something with none of them. The lead skill's full instructions go in; every other skill contributes one line describing what it can do, plus its tools and its panels. You pick the leader. The order is deterministic, so the same loadout assembles the same way on every machine, every launch.

      What happens when two skills disagree?

      Some pairs are simply refused — the free Kitchen Timer and the full Cooking Assistant are the same muscle at different sizes, so the app will not let you carry both. Where two skills touch the same list, exactly one of them owns it and the other reads or appends; the app decides, deterministically, and tells you which. Panels that are genuinely identical get merged rather than drawn twice.

      Can I write my own skill?

      Not yet, honestly. The catalogue is first-party and curated so that the safety story above is actually true. A closed-beta SDK goes to a handful of design partners first, and a public one — with the same certification pipeline and a revenue share — comes after that. We would rather ship a small shelf that works than a large one that does not.

      Is it as good as ChatGPT?

      At answering anything, no, and it is not pretending to. It is a small model on your processor. What it has instead is a timer that is already running, a list that stays on screen, a unit switch that works mid-sentence, and no internet connection. For a structured job you repeat every week, that is a better tool. For an essay about the Peloponnesian War, open a browser.
